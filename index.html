Sector Overview Buy-side primer

Indian defence runs on government rules and slow government buying — not on normal market forces.

Indian defence is a chain of companies that build weapons, ships, aircraft, and electronics — but only the government buys from them. So policy decisions, slow tendering, and rules about how much must be made in India shape every company’s fortunes more than market demand. When picking stocks, what matters most is which buying rule applies, how much value is genuinely added in India, whether the company can actually deliver on time, what’s still imported, and whether reported profits turn into real cash.

Plain-English explainerExplain

Imagine a country where only one customer exists for every weapons company — the army & defence ministry. That’s India.

Because of that, three things matter much more than a private-sector business:

  • Government rules decide who can supply what (these are called DAP categories).
  • Tendering takes years, so an “order” today may become revenue 3-5 years later.
  • India still imports critical parts (engines, sensors, advanced chips) so a delay overseas can stop production at home.

If you understand these three things, the rest of this dashboard makes sense.

Why this number is misleadingExplain

The headline ₹6.81 lakh-crore sounds huge — but only the gold slice (about ₹1.49 L Cr, or 22%) actually buys new aircraft, ships, missiles, radars and other equipment. That is what defence companies sell into.

The dark navy slice (74%) is mostly salaries, pensions, fuel, ammunition stocking, and base maintenance. It funds the armed forces but flows very little to private industry.

The cyan slice is DRDO, which designs new equipment in-house — sometimes handed to private firms to manufacture later.

Bottom line for an investor: when somebody says “defence budget grew 9.5%”, what you actually want to know is whether the capital portion grew, and whether contracts are getting signed and delivered.

What is SRIJAN?Explain

SRIJAN is a government website where each defence-PSU (HAL, BEL, BDL etc.) publishes a list of items it currently imports. Indian companies can browse the list, register, and offer to make those items locally.

Why it matters for investors: every item moved from the “imported” column to the “made-in-India” column is a new business opportunity for some Indian supplier. As of Feb 2025, ~14,000 of 38,000+ listed items had been replaced by domestic alternatives.

What is iDEX?Explain

iDEX is the defence ministry’s startup challenge programme. The army or navy posts a real problem (“we need a counter-drone system that fits on a jeep”), startups compete to solve it, and the winners get a development contract plus a path to actual orders.

Read the funnel: 549 problems have been posted, 619 startups have engaged with them, and 430 have actually signed contracts. It’s the cleanest way to spot emerging private defence-tech companies before they show up on stock screens.

Why FDI rules matterExplain

Foreign companies (Lockheed, Airbus, Rafael etc.) can’t freely buy Indian defence companies. Government rules cap how much they can own.

The automatic route means a foreign investor can buy up to 74% of an Indian defence firm without prior approval. To go above 74% (up to 100%), they need explicit government clearance.

What this means for investors: liberalised rules invite global JVs and technology tie-ups, which in turn boost the order pipeline for Indian listed names. But each large foreign deal is still government-gated, so progress is uneven.

01 The headline budget number lies

Money allocated isn’t money spent. Between the budget announcement and a company’s revenue line are years of approvals, trials, milestone payments, and inspection sign-offs.

02 The “how” of buying matters more than “how much”

The government has 8 different ways to buy — pure-Indian, joint-venture, direct import, leasing. Each one decides who can bid, how much must be made in India, and how good the margins are.

03 Whole-product makers and parts suppliers are different businesses

The companies that build the whole jet/ship/missile have scale and visibility. The smaller suppliers who sell sub-components into them often have better margins — but each depends on a few customers.

04 India makes ships and electronics well, but still imports the hard stuff

Strong: warship hulls, missile bodies, ammunition, integration of electronics. Still imported: aero engines, advanced missile seekers, high-end chips, infrared detectors.

05 A pile of orders is worthless if they can’t be delivered

Companies brag about huge “order book” numbers. What matters is the executable piece — orders where the customer has funding, the factory is ready, and parts are available.

06 Reported profit ≠ cash in the bank

The government is a slow-paying customer. Stuck receivables, growing inventory, and delayed inspections can leave a company with healthy reported profit but very weak cash flow.

07 Don’t lump all defence stocks together

Aerospace, shipbuilding, electronics, missiles, drones, and materials are six different industries that happen to share one customer. They have different economics, different risks, and need different valuation approaches. A single “defence theme” bucket destroys signal.

All seven findings, in one paragraphExplain

If you remember only one thing from this dashboard, it’s this:

The Indian defence sector looks simple from the outside (“budget went up, defence stocks should rise”) but is structurally complicated. The government is the only customer; it has eight different ways of buying; tendering takes years; many critical parts are still imported; payment is slow; and the companies under the “defence” label do completely different jobs — building aircraft, supplying chips, making explosives, writing software.

So instead of buying a “defence basket”, the analyst’s job is to look inside each company: what kind of contracts, how much is made in India, can they actually deliver, what could break execution, and does the cash arrive when promised.

Budget & Spending Total budget · ceiling of demand

The budget is the ceiling of what could be spent — not the contract, not the delivery, not the cash.

A budget allocation is just a number on paper at the start of the year. Between that number and a defence company’s revenue line are years of approvals, technical trials, contract negotiations, milestone payments, and inspection sign-offs. To see whether money is actually flowing, watch the DAC approvals, new Positive List additions, SRIJAN closures, and defence exports — not just the budget headline.

Plain-English explainerExplain

Think of the defence budget like a household putting aside ₹1,00,000 in January for a possible kitchen renovation. That’s the ceiling — the most that could be spent.

But none of it leaves the bank until: the family decides what to renovate (capability gap), gets quotes (tender), picks a contractor (commercial bid), signs the contract, and then pays in stages as work happens. Skip a step, the money sits in the account.

So when newspapers headline “Defence budget grew 9.5%”, an investor still has to check — in this order — whether capital grew (procurement money), whether DAC is approving new programmes, whether contracts are being signed, and whether companies are actually being paid.

Is +9.53% a big number?Explain

+9.53% sounds healthy, but it’s a nominal growth rate — before adjusting for inflation. With Indian inflation typically running 4-6%, the real (inflation-adjusted) increase is closer to 4-5%.

More importantly, growth in the total budget doesn’t automatically mean more business for defence companies. If the increase is mostly going to higher salaries or pensions, listed defence companies see no benefit. What matters is whether the capital outlay grew at the same rate — which is a separate number you have to dig out of the Budget documents.

Why DRDO’s growth matters more than total budget growthExplain

DRDO is India’s defence R&D body. They design new missiles, radars, electronic warfare systems, sensors, and strategic platforms in-house, then hand the designs to public-sector or private companies to build at scale.

So the DRDO budget is a forward-looking signal. Today’s DRDO money is roughly tomorrow’s production contracts — for the manufacturers DRDO works with (HAL, BEL, BDL, plus growing private partners like Astra and Data Patterns).

+12.41% growth (vs +9.53% for the total budget) means R&D priority is increasing faster than the overall budget — a positive medium-term signal for indigenous-design names.

Why allocation isn’t revenue

8 hurdles between a budget line and a company’s P&L

  1. 1
    A service spots a gap — Army / Navy / Air Force says “we need more of X”
  2. 2
    Requirement is written down — specifications, how many, by when
  3. 3
    Buying rule chosen — under DAP (Indian-only, joint with foreign, import, lease …)
  4. 4
    Tender (RFP) goes out — eligible companies submit bids
  5. 5
    Trials and technical testing — biggest cause of delay; can take 1-3 years on its own
  6. 6
    Commercial bids opened — price competition only between companies that passed trials
  7. 7
    Contract negotiation & approvals — L1 disputes, legal review, Cabinet sign-off for big deals
  8. 8
    Production, delivery, milestone payments — revenue and cash come in over multiple years as deliveries clear inspection
Why does this take 5+ years?Explain

A defence purchase isn’t buying a phone. The army has to be sure the equipment works in the actual battlefield, the price is competitive (a lowest-bidder dispute can stall a tender for a year), and the procurement is legal and approved at the highest level (sometimes Cabinet).

Average timeline from a service identifying a gap to a defence company recognising revenue is 5-10 years. The big platform deals (submarines, fighters) often take longer.

For an investor, this means: a single contract win shows up as revenue gradually, not in one quarter. And announcement of an order is often a poor predictor of when cash arrives.

The four-question test

D
Demand

Armed forces define operational need; the MoD acquisition system processes procurement. — demand is state-controlled.

R
R&D

DRDO remains central in missiles, radars, EW, sensors, strategic systems — but private R&D is increasingly relevant in electronics, drones, ammunition, precision subsystems.

M
Manufacture

DPSUs still dominate large platforms and many strategic programs; private firms increasingly visible in electronics, drones, ammunition, structures, precision subsystems.

$
Capture

Economics flow unevenly. Platform primes capture scale and visibility; electronics/system houses often capture better value-add; build-to-print vendors get lower pricing power. Imported critical content can cap domestic margin expansion.

Don't accept “Indian” at face value

Three questions before believing the indigenisation claim

  1. Q1
    What is the domestic content percentage?

    Headline category alone tells you preference, not realised local content.

  2. Q2
    Is the high-value content local or imported?

    A locally fabricated shell with imported guts is not a value-add story.

  3. Q3
    Are engines, seekers, chips, RF modules, optronics, or propulsion still foreign-sourced?

    These are the structural ceilings on margin and resilience.

What this dashboard won’t tell you

Treat the dashboard as a primer, not an oracle. The following are deliberately not in here — you will need external work to close these:

  • ▸ Live financials and valuations — revenue, margins, P/E, market cap. Pull from latest exchange filings and concalls.
  • ▸ Order-book size and quarterly inflow per company. Track via BSE/NSE corporate announcements weekly.
  • ▸ Programme-level milestones — Tejas delivery rate, AMCA stage, P75-I status. Cross-check via PIB MoD releases and DRDO updates.
  • ▸ Live order-conversion ratios: how much of each company’s gross order book is actually executable in 12-24 months. Ask management on every concall.

Headline numbers (MoD allocation, DRDO, SRIJAN, FDI) live in the Overview KPI ribbon and the Budget tab. No need to repeat them here.

Final investor takeaway

Indian defence is one of the few sectors where policy creates multi-year demand visibility — and one of the few where revenue recognition, testing, milestone billing, imported content, and procurement delays make growth highly uneven. The investors who compound here are the ones who do not mistake announcements for revenue, who separate the basket by tier and bucket, and who watch cash conversion alongside reported profit.
